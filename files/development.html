Debunking Myth about Gun System My recent and past investigations about gun system issues since its ultimate version was released, despite many clips and everything, have all demonstrated the following: there are no issues, it is flawless. The problem is always coming from the human, or eventually, server lag, but there's not much we can do about this. Analyzing, frame by frame, clips that were ever sent to me always revealed the same thing: shot was slightly missed, either because of aim even by one pixel, or because of spread, and hitreg behaved correctly. I have also noticed a lot of cliches about this gun system, in particular regarding the visible bullets. So, I'm gonna try to explain you how it works and why it cannot behave wrongfully. Roblox is unfortunately a platform where players' positions are badly synced for all clients because each client owns the position of its player. This means that the position information has to travel twice through the internet. First, from the player's client to the Roblox server, then from the server to your client. Therefore, you need to understand that everyone doesn't see exactly the same thing on their screen. We can say that there are different sources of "truth". Due to this, there is no way to make a "perfect gun system". All systems have to sacrifice something, but this approach is probably the best option for our case. In technical terms, this gun system uses "client-sided raycasting", which contrasts with "server-sided raycasting" and "emulated raycasting". What does this mean? On the second your mouse button is pressed down, the gun system will instantly, on your computer, cast a ray (draw a straight line) starting from the tip of your weapon towards the "mouse target". But before doing so, the bullet's direction will receive some spread depending on your weapon. We will talk about spread later on. (edited)
O5-5 03-Aug-21 06:20 PM
The fact that this process of raycasting is done on the client of the shooter means that the truth that is used for calculating the shot's landing point is what the shooter sees on their screen. This guarantees that if the shot lands on the target on your screen, the hit is registered. However, this has a downside: on the target's screen, they might have a slightly different position in which you would have been unable to reach them, but they will still get damaged. Another point is that this gun system uses "pure" raycasting, instead of emulated raycasting. This means that there is no delay between the moment where you click and the moment where the shot's landing point is calculated. Bullets that you see on your screen are purely aesthetic. They follow the same trajectory as the raycast and will stop at the same landing location. They are very slow, yes. The point is that you can see them, but they don't determine where your shot lands. And in fact, you can often see people taking damage before even being hit by this artefact bullet. Now let's talk about spread. On some other gun systems (most will know what I'm referring to), these fake bullets do not exist. And because of that, people tend to think that there is no spread, but there actually is. You just don't notice it. The spread is a random value in degrees ranging from minus max to max, where max is the spread value that you can find on the guns spreadsheet. This means that your bullet will always stay within a cone whose top angle is twice this max value. The spread is applied on your client right before raycasting and may genuinely affect you shot's landing point, in particular in long range. (edited)
O5-5 03-Aug-21 06:29 PM
It is possible to get an idea of how far your shot may land away from your cursor using trigonometry formulas. You just need to consider the distance between you and the supposed target, and know the max spread of your weapon. Use the following formula: distance * tan(maxSpread), in degrees of course. For instance, with a distance of 50 studs (medium quarter) and a spread of 2.5 degrees (R870), the maximum stray distance is about 2.2 studs, which is a lot. Hitreg issues have been most reported on shotguns so let's talk about them. It is important to know that the gun system uses a realistic pellet system, which means that it fires 12 pellets for shotguns and handles each of them as an individual bullet, with their own spread amount, own raycasting, etc. This is really important because it reflects best reality and accurate hitreg. I remember some MTF asking for the shotgun spread to be tightened. This was done, but one consequence of this is that they now behave more like snipers or actually any other gun with single pellet. In close quarter, missing the target by even one pixel will result in most pellets missing the target. I hope that I have enlightened you about this gun system and proved it is flawless. It doesn't mean that unexpected things never happen. There are always server lag, client lag, invisible things floating here and there that may sometimes grasp your shots, but there is no systematic problem. If you have any questions, feel free to ask me. (edited)
